DNA, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is the hereditary material in humans and almost all other organisms. Nearly every cell in a persons body has the same DNA. Most DNA is located in the cell nucleus (where it is called nuclear DNA), but a small amount of DNA can also be found in the mitochondria (where it is called mitochondrial DNA or mtDNA).

Source: DNA Encyclopedia and DNA Structure

The information in DNA is stored as a code made up of four chemical bases: adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), and thymine (T). Human DNA consists of about 3 billion bases, and more than 99 percent of those bases are the same in all people. The order, or sequence, of these bases determines the information available for building and maintaining an organism, similar to the way in which letters of the alphabet appear in a certain order to form words and sentences.

DNA bases pair up with each other, A with T and C with G, to form units called base pairs. Each base is also attached to a sugar molecule and a phosphate molecule. Together, a base, sugar, and phosphate are called a nucleotide. Nucleotides are arranged in two long strands that form a spiral called a double helix. The structure of the double helix is somewhat like a ladder, with the base pairs forming the ladders rungs and the sugar and phosphate molecules forming the vertical sidepieces of the ladder.

An important property of DNA is that it can replicate, or make copies of itself. Each strand of DNA in the double helix can serve as a pattern for duplicating the sequence of bases. This is critical when cells divide because each new cell needs to have an exact copy of the DNA present in the old cell.

DNA is the acronym for Deoxyribonucleic acid.
It is one of the two forms of Nucleic Acid present in living beings the other being RNA or Ribonucleic Acid.
The definition of DNA varies with every perspective so I would like to break it down from all angles:
 
Chemically, DNA is a negatively charged molecule composed of two bipolymer strands. Each of the bipolymer strands contains nucleotides or nitrogen-containing four nucleobases [Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Thymine (T) and Cytosine (C)], a monsaccharide sugar or deoxyribose to be specific and a phosphate group. The nucleotides are joined to one another in a chain by covalent bonds between the sugar of one nucleotide and the phosphate of the next, resulting in an alternating sugar-phosphate backbone. As a rule of thumb, A always pair with T and G always pairs with C thus giving bonding to the two bipolymer strands making it a helical structure.



Image Courtsey: Genetics - CellUniverse

Biologically, DNA is the carrier of genetic material. Genes, present as series of ATGC sequences and packed as helical structures are coherently called as DNA. Humans have approximately 20,000-25,000 protein coding genes, thus naturally all this information encoded in the form of DNA will consume a lot of physical space in the cell. Hence to conserve this space, long stretches of DNA are packed up into bundles by special histone proteins. These bundles of DNA are chromosomes. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes accountable for the protein coding genes as well as other non-coding genetic elements. This DNA is passed from one generation to the next via reproduction thus accounting for similar traits in parents and offspring. Also this DNA is replicated (during cell division) and unfolded to obtain information encoded in genes via biologically processes known as transcription and translation to make proteins and take important decisions of the cell. DNA is also responsible for introducing mutations in the species by random rearrangement of gene sequences or recombination during reproduction.
 


Image Courtsey: Ethics of Genomics

The importance of DNA is immense and cannot be underestimated. DNA structure was elucidated in 1953 by Dr. James Watson and Dr. Francis Crick with the help of Rosalind Franklin via X-ray Crystallography of the DNA molecule. DNA is not only the genetic carrier of humans or animals but also plants, bacteria and a variety of viruses.

Originally Answered: What exactly is DNA? How does it work?
DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid. As mentioned in the answer by Rohit Nair, it is the hereditary material in almost all organisms. It is a double-helical structure (Francis & Crick, 1953).


What is DNA made of?
DNA consists of units called deoxyribonucleotides. Each deoxyribonucleotide, in turn, is made up of the following three components: a deoxyribose sugar, a nitrogenous base (adenine, guanine, cytosine or thymine) and a phosphate group. Thus, DNA consists of a long chain of deoxyribonucleotides linked together in a particular sequence which codes for genetic information.



In eukaryotes:


DNA is packaged into several distinct structures known as chromosomes. Each chromosome represents one long, tightly packed DNA molecule. Humans have 46 chromosomes. Thus, if we were to compare the genetic information of a human being with an encyclopaedia,  the 46 chromosomes could be compared to 46 volumes of the encyclopaedia!


So, what does DNA do?
DNA acts as a stable molecule to be passed on from one generation to the next, keeping intact the genetic information essential for sustaining life.

How is the information contained in DNA utilised?
DNA undergoes a process called transcription inside the nucleus of a cell, in which one strand of the double-helix acts as a template for the synthesis of mRNA (messenger RNA, or ribonucleic acid). This mRNA now migrates to the cytoplasm of the cell, where it undergoes a process called translation, which leads to the formation of polypeptide chains from mRNA which give rise to various proteins. The information contained in the DNA helps decide which protein is to be formed. This is known as the Central dogma of molecular biology (Crick, 1956). Proteins are very important molecules within the cell, and play a myriad of roles (see Protein Function). These, with the aid of other molecules in an organism's body help in maintaining the various metabolic processes essential to sustain life.


In prokaryotes:


In prokaryotes, which lack a specific nucleus, DNA is contained within the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid. Here, transcription and translation both occur in the cytoplasm, due to lack of its compartmentalisation. Because of this feature, it is also possible for transcription and translation to occur simultaneously in prokaryotes (whereas this is not the case in eukaryotes).

  1. Eukaryotes have organelles which come with their own DNA, the mitochondria and the chroroplast. Moreover they have their own genetic code and their own machinery to synthesize proteins. This goes on to show that DNA retains its individual identity even after millions of years (this assumes that mito. and chloro. are unicellular organisms which were integrated into the early eukaryotes).
  2. DNA comes in various forms and sizes. The 3 classical forms of DNA are A, B and Z DNA. In the body DNA can form many secondary structures like hairpin, triplex etc. These structures are required to maintain expression levels of protein i.e. proteins can distinguish these these structures and use that information to perform various functions.
  3. Almost 70% of the human genome is made of junk DNA. Junk DNA is so called because it codes for no specific proteins. They are mostly transposable elements. Transposable elements are DNA containing specific sequences (more precisely inverted repeats). DNA uses its minions (proteins) to cut or copy these elements and pasting it somewhere else in the genome leading to genetic variation.
  4. There is a specific human locus called the Rosa locus which is constitutively expressed. Given how much energy goes into maintaining the whole machinery, why bother having it? Or is it that there is a positive selection in favor of it?

There are many more fascinating things about DNA.

Originally Answered: What is the mean of [DNA]?
A nucleic acid that carries the genetic information in the cell and is capable of self-replication and synthesis of RNA. DNA consists of two long chains of nucleotides twisted into a double helix and joined by hydrogen bonds between the complementary bases adenine and thymine or cytosine and guanine. The sequence of nucleotides determines individual hereditary characteristics.
(Life Sciences & Allied Applications / Biochemistry) deoxyribonucleic acid; a nucleic acid that is the main constituent of the chromosomes of all organisms (except some viruses). The DNA molecule consists of two polynucleotide chains in the form of a double helix, containing phosphate and the sugar deoxyribose and linked by hydrogen bonds between the complementary bases adenine and thymine or cytosine and guanine. DNA is self-replicating, plays a central role in protein synthesis, and is responsible for the transmission of hereditary characteristics from parents to offspring See also genetic code
