Originally Answered: Is intelligence mostly genetic?

Highly. Per the American Psychological Association, the heritability of IQ (as we measure intelligence currently) is estimated at around 75% by adulthood. This means that most of the difference between person As IQ and Person Bs IQ is attributable to their genetic differences (genes inherited by their parents).

These estimates began with some of the first research done on intelligence, or g, as a construct of general cognitive abilities that underlie most academic tasks. Beginning with family studies (twin studies, adoption studies), the heritability of intelligence is now, I believe, mostly confined to molecular genetic research.

However, one should keep in mind that the word most is important. There is still that 25% (or more) attributable to non-genetic factors. Environmental factors can, and do, exert an influence on the development of ones intelligence. Unfortunately, this tends to be mostly one-way. That is, poor environmental influences can have significantly negative effects on intellectual development while positive (or neutral) environmental influences have minor to no significant effect on ones IQ.

Answered Jan 9, 2018 · Author has 7.7k answers and 32.6m answer views

Half.

I'm not being facetious either. Based on wide ranging and long-term studies, scientists conclude that somewhere around 50% of the natural variation in IQ can be attributed to genetics, the rest being due to other factors, primarily environmental.

What that means for you personally is more complex. Coming from a family with high IQ's, the odds are pretty good that you'll have a high IQ as well, since you share their genes, and you've probably largely shared their environment. But those observations are statistical. It means that a higher-IQ genetic background increases the odds of having a high IQ, but guarantees nothing. Someone can be born of unremarkable parents and have a genius IQ, one can be born of intellectuals and be mentally disabled. The world is a complicated place, biology is especially complicated, and intelligence is insanely complicated. Your IQ could deviate anywhere on the map. The farther aware from your family's IQ range, the less likely it is, but anything's possible.

Thing is, IQ tests and MENSA memberships mean very little in the real world. I'd be more concerned about academic performance, the skills you've acquired, the knowledge you've gained and the experiences you've had. Those things are pretty good indicators of how much you can accomplish. Your membership in MENSA (or lack thereof) really isn't.

Thanks for the a2a. There are several points to make here.

1. Your original question was "Is iq genetic or is it determined by various differing circumstances or both?" In my view both.

My model is the plastic bag; your plastic bag has a maximal volume, meaning you were born with a maximally attainable IQ. Your society and family situation may detract from your maximum (punch holes in the plastic bag and step on it). Conversely there is probably not much anyone can do to push the limit further up (no IQ drug).

So in my view, maximal IQ is hereditary, but measured IQ is both hereditary and societal (and depends on your personal history). IQ tests implicitly test the society as well as the person. (To be clear, I state this as my opinion, not as a fact, so others may disagree.)

2. Mensa accepts applicants whose IQ is two standard deviations (SD) above the average. If you express it this way, you should not be mistaken.

Most IQ scales use average IQ = 100. This is a choice, not a definition. SD varies. For instance, some use 24, some use 15 or 16. If you are measured on a scale with SD=15, then IQ=130 will suffice. If SD=24, then you need IQ=148. On a scale where average is 0, and SD=1, you would need 2. Nobody uses that, but they should. It would be just as valid.

The test just measures how you perform, i.e. how many points you score in the test. For instance, 15 out of 20 possible points. 15 is called your raw score, usually. That result is then converted to the appropriate scale. It doesn't matter which one they use, it's just inches versus centimeters. It sounds like your relatives were converted to a scale with ID=24. If your result is on a scale with SD=15, and theirs is SD=24 (not likely), then your score of 135 might equate their 156 (the exact measure is probably something slightly different). I would expect your score will be measured by the same scale so that they compare directly. So let's use that one from now on.

3. Suppose your mother's (unkown) IQ is 156 just like your father's. Then your expected average IQ would be (156+156+100+100)/4 = 128. (The following calculations are independent of the value of SD.) I don't remember where I picked up that formula, but when I find the source, I will have a lot of places to plant it on Quora a.o.

The two times 100 in this formula explain what is called the regression towards the mean; I saw somewhere that if it weren't so, if the true formula were (156+156)/2, then the variance of the measured IQ would tend to fall to zero over time, and in the end everybody would have an IQ of exactly 100. I am not really sure of that as I write this. But this is the correct formula, and it means parents with an extremely high IQ will be expected to produce children with an IQ that is high, but not quite so extremely high. Similarly, some parents of IQ may produce children with a high or extremely high IQ, just by chance.

But this is an expected average, and some children will have a higher, some will have a lower IQ.

4. We don't know your mother's IQ, but we know your sister's, and it is high. I suppose somewhere there are formulae for calculating the expected IQ of the parents of someone with an IQ=156 plus a way to estimate your mother's IQ from your sister's and your father's, but I don't know those, and working backwards from the first formula would be cumbersome (perhaps involving some gymnastics with Bayes' formula, or some such, I don't know at this moment). My intuition tells me: Your mother's IQ is probably above average. So let's put it tentatively at 128, why not.

If your mother's IQ is 128, then her children with your father would be expected to have an IQ around (128+156+100+100)/4 = 135. Even though your sister actually had an IQ of 156, this result could simply be an unlikely, but not improbable event. In this formula it is exactly as probable as having an IQ of 114.

Your IQ, governed by the same math would probably be 135, but it could be 156 or 114. And all of this hangs on your mother's IQ being 128 which is just my guess.

5. The test is age-adjusted. If you are very young, and you score max points, you might actually get a measured IQ that permits you to enter Mensa, whereas a middle-aged person with the same raw score might not be able to do that.

6. Note that many tests only measure so high, they have a maximum. They might simply record your result as ">156", and the "real" IQ which cannot be measured by that test, is higher. This might mean that your father and sister's IQs are really higher, so your mother's would have to be higher, and therefore you would have a good chance of passing the test.

(I think the tests are designed this way, partly because it is difficult to measure really high IQs, and partly because the customer (Mensa) only asks whether your IQ is high enough, not how high it is.)

Well, I just went through some scientific articles for you. This one is good: http://www.cell.com/current-biol...

Let me summarize for you. We will assume that IQ is a good measure of intelligence (but is it really?)

  1. What is intelligence? According toLinda Gottfredsons: Intelligence is a very general mental capability that, among other things, involves the ability to reason, plan, solve problems, think abstractly, comprehend complex ideas, learn quickly and learn from experience. It is not merely book learning, a narrow academic skill, or test-taking smarts. Rather, it reflects a broader and deeper capability for comprehending our surroundings catching on, making sense of things, or figuring out what to do.
  2. Is intelligence hereditary? From doing tests on twins and adopted children we can try to establish if there is any hereditary factor to intelligence. The percentage of the variation in intelligence accounted for by genetic causes is usually given at about 50%. With regard to the environment, twin studies suggest that the contribution of shared environment to intelligence differences is small, even negligible, by adulthood, and that that which is nongenetic is largely due to non-shared environment and measurement error. Interestingly enough, there is about a 30% correlation between intelligence test scores and overall brain size. As yet, it is not understood what it is about bigger brains that is associated with being brighter.

In answer to your initial question, it is very likely that your test will show an IQ close to your mothers and sisters. It is hard to say exactly what will be the range and with what confidence, but I would expect somewhere higher than 130.

I'm guessing that your sister is your elder?  It is common for the oldest child in a family to seem like the most intelligent.  It is not statistically expected that they actually are.

IQ seems to be about like height.  That is, you are likely to be within fairly close range of your parents.  If your mother was especially not intelligent, then you would have more statistical chance of not having an IQ as high as your father. 

You have a parent and a sibling with high IQ's, therefore, you have a high likelihood of also having a high IQ.  "Regression to the mean" suggests that your IQ would likely be a few points lower, only because it is unlikely that a third datapoint (you) would be the same or higher.  But it is entirely possible that you could be a point or two higher.

It also depends on your mother's IQ.

An IQ of 156 is so far into the 2% level that Mensa requires, that chances are you could get ten points lower than that, and still qualify for Mensa.

I hope that in the future, you will find your own life to be comfortable, and not be in such deep competition with your father and sister.

Originally Answered: Is intelligence mostly genetic?

This is a broad answer but it is also the correct answer. The answer is a combination of nature and nurture. If your parents are both gifted (nature) and you are brought up in an intellectually stimulating environment (nurture), there is a good chance of being intellectually superior. We all know that Mendel fudged his genetic data on pea plants. Genetic variables allow anything to be possible. Parents could both have IQs three or four standard deviations above the norm and produce children who have average or below intelligence. The interesting part is when things are not positive in both nature and nurture. If gifted parents do not stimulate their children, the children may not be superior. If a child only has the genetic potential to be average but is brought up in an intellectually stimulating environment, they may have a superior IQ. Much human potential is gained during early childhood. This is all a generalization but generally correct. We have all heard of child prodigies who came out of nowhere. The parents were not noted for superiority and their upbringing was not exceptional but the child is gifted.

Very.

The heritability of IQ is, according to Wikipedia, between 57% and 73% with some more-recent estimates as high as 80% and 86%.

Genome-wide association studies have identified inherited genome sequence differences that account for 20% of the 50% of the genetic variation that contributes to heritability.

IQ developes throughout the childhood and youth and stabilizes in young adulthood. IQ goes from being weakly correlated with genetics, for children, to being strongly correlated with genetics for late teens and adults. The heritability of IQ increases with age and reaches an asymptote at 1820 years of age and continues at that level well into adulthood.

See also the caveats.
